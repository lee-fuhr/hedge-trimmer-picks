How to choose the right hedge trimmer

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For most homeowners, a cordless electric hedge trimmer is the best balance of power, convenience, and low maintenance, just be sure to match the blade length to the size of your hedges and buy into a battery system you can use with other outdoor tools. Focus on battery voltage (higher is better for thicker growth), blade gap (larger gaps tackle heavier branches), and overall weight if you’ll be trimming for more than 20 minutes at a time.

Why choose cordless over corded or gas?

Cordless trimmers give you the freedom to roam around the yard without dragging an extension cord or dealing with a gas engine’s noise, smell, and maintenance. They start instantly with a squeeze of the trigger and need far less upkeep than gas models, no oil changes, no fuel mixing, no spark plugs. A corded trimmer offers unlimited runtime and is often cheaper, but the cord seriously limits reach and can be a nuisance to manage. Gas trimmers still win on raw power for very thick, overgrown brush, but for routine hedge shaping in a typical suburban yard, a modern high-voltage cordless model is more than enough and much more pleasant to use.

What blade length and tooth gap do you need?

Blade length typically ranges from about 18 to 30 inches. Shorter blades (18–22 inches) are lighter and easier to maneuver for detailed shaping and smaller shrubs. Longer blades (24–30 inches) let you trim a larger section per pass, which speeds up work on long hedges but can feel tiring overhead. Tooth gap, the space between the cutting teeth, determines the maximum branch thickness the trimmer can cut. A gap of around ¾ inch is standard for light duty; 1 inch or more handles thicker branches up to about 1.2 inches. If your hedges have old woody growth, aim for a larger gap. Dual-action blades (both blades moving) reduce vibration and give cleaner cuts than single-action designs.

How important is battery system compatibility?

Hugely important. Most major brands, DeWalt, Ego, Ryobi, Milwaukee, Makita, Greenworks, Black+Decker, sell a range of outdoor tools that share the same batteries: trimmers, blowers, chainsaws, string trimmers, and more. Buying into one system early gives you flexibility and cost savings, since you only need a few batteries and chargers for the whole lineup. For a hedge trimmer, a 40V or 56V battery usually delivers better cutting power and longer runtime than the 18V/20V platforms, though those can still work for small jobs. Before picking a trimmer, think about what other tools you might want and pick a reputable system with good availability. Check user reviews for real-world runtime on that specific battery size.

What about weight and ergonomics?

Weight matters because you’ll be holding the trimmer out in front of you for minutes at a time. Most cordless hedge trimmers weigh between 5 and 8 pounds with the battery. Lighter models under 6 pounds are noticeably nicer for overhead work and long sessions; heavier models can fatigue your arms quickly, though they often have higher power and larger batteries. Look for a comfortable grip with rubberized rubber overmold, a wrap-around handle (especially one that rotates 90 degrees for vertical cuts), and a well-balanced design that doesn’t tip forward heavily. Some trimmers include a pivoting head or adjustable rear handle to reduce wrist strain. Testing the trigger and safety switch reach in a store (if possible) is really helpful.

Do you need a brushless motor?

Brushless motors are more efficient, produce more torque per watt, last longer, and generally run cooler than brushed motors. Nearly all mid-to-high-end cordless hedge trimmers now use brushless motors, and they’re worth the extra cost if you trim more than a few times a season. Brushed motors are still found in budget-friendly models. They work fine for light use, but they draw more current (draining batteries faster) and eventually the brushes wear out. If you plan to keep the trimmer for years or regularly tackle thicker growth, prioritize brushless for better runtime and reliability.

What safety features should you look for?

At minimum, a two-handed operation with a lock-on trigger switch that requires both hands to be on the handles before the blades move. This prevents accidental startup. A blade brake (stops the blades within a second of releasing the trigger) is a real safety plus, especially when moving between plants or setting the trimmer down. Some models also include a wraparound handguard to protect fingers from branches or from slipping forward. For extended reach, an optional extension pole attachment can let you trim overhead without a ladder, but only use one designed for that specific trimmer. Always wear gloves and eye protection regardless of safety features.

Frequently asked questions

How long does a cordless hedge trimmer battery last?

Runtime depends on battery capacity (amp-hours) and the thickness of what you’re cutting. A typical 2.5–4 Ah battery on a 40V or 56V trimmer can run 30–60 minutes of continuous light trimming, or about half that on heavy growth. Most people can finish a normal yard in one charge if they buy the larger battery option.

Can you cut thick branches with a cordless hedge trimmer?

Yes, but only up to a certain thickness, usually ½ inch to 1 inch depending on the blade gap and power. For branches thicker than 1 inch, you’re better off using a pruning saw or loppers. Trying to force a hedge trimmer through heavy wood can damage the blades and stall the motor.

Is a cordless hedge trimmer as powerful as a gas one?

For typical home hedges, boxwoods, privet, yew, small evergreens, a modern high-voltage cordless trimmer (40V–80V) matches or exceeds gas for cutting speed and cleanliness. For very thick, woody overgrowth that’s been neglected for years, a gas model still has an edge in sustained torque, but the gap is closing fast.

What’s the difference between single-action and dual-action blades?

Single-action blades have one fixed blade and one moving blade; dual-action blades have both blades moving in opposite directions. Dual-action reduces vibration significantly, your hands and arms feel much less fatigue, and usually gives a cleaner cut. The trade-off is slightly more weight and higher cost, but the comfort gain is well worth it.

Can I use a hedge trimmer to trim hedges in rough shapes like spirals?

You can, but a hedge trimmer is best for straight lines and gentle curves. For fine topiary or spiral shapes, you’re better off using manual shears for precise cuts. The long blade of a power trimmer makes it hard to follow tight turns without accidentally over-trimming.

In shortPicking the right cordless hedge trimmer really comes down to three things: the battery system you want to invest in, the blade length that fits your hedge sizes, and a weight you can comfortably handle for a full session. Spend a few minutes considering your yard’s needs and the battery ecosystem you already own (or plan to own), and you’ll end up with a tool that makes trimming feel less like a chore and more like a quick tidy-up.
